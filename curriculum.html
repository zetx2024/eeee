Module 1: Foundations of Academic Writing

Complete, hands-on instruction on research and review paper creation, submission rules, and structure.

1.1 Research Paper: Research Paper Writing

Learning outcomes, guidelines, sections & references
3500+ words

Learning Outcomes

Students will learn to design, structure, and prepare a full-length research paper suitable for submission and competitions.

General Guidelines (MANDATORY)

  • Minimum 3500 words total.
  • Use the YRJ Microsoft Word template and submit in .docx format.
  • Include all text, figures, and tables with clear figure captions.
  • English language only.
  • Mandatory IEEE style for in-text citations and reference list.

Submission of Versions

  • Upload a complete manuscript (.docx).
  • Upload a text-only version (.docx) (figures removed or placeholders).
  • Upload a figures-only version (.docx) (all figures and captions compiled).

Title Page & Abstract

  • Title Page — Title (≤20 words), author names, affiliations, and credit to mentors/collaborators.
  • Abstract — Concise overview (≤300 words): significance, defined problem, brief methodology, key findings, and broader impact.

Core Sections

  • Introduction: Set the stage, incorporate literature, articulate objectives/questions/hypotheses, preview conclusions.
  • Literature Review: Comprehensive review, theoretical frameworks, key concepts, and contributions to knowledge.
  • Methods: Research design, sampling strategy (justify sample size), data collection, data analysis methods and statistical tools.
  • Results: Present findings objectively — use tables/figures/charts, descriptive & inferential statistics, properly labeled visuals.
  • Discussion: Interpret findings relative to questions and literature, address limitations and biases, discuss implications.
  • Conclusion: Summarize outcomes, significance, contributions, and future research directions.
  • References: IEEE style — every in-text citation must have a reference list entry.

1.2 Review Paper: Review Paper Writing

Synthesis, methods for reviews, and publication standards
3500+ words

Learning Outcomes

Students will learn how to critically evaluate and synthesize the existing literature to produce a high-quality review paper.

General Guidelines

  • Minimum 3500 words.
  • Use YRJ Word template, .docx submission, English only.
  • Mandatory IEEE style for citations.
  • Submit complete manuscript, text-only version, and figures-only version separately.

Abstract (≤350 words)

Summarize the importance of the review, define its purpose/question, highlight the main hypothesis/focus, outline findings, and underscore implications.

Sections & Methods

  • Introduction: Introduce the scientific context, background references, research purpose/hypothesis/rationale, and a glimpse of conclusions.
  • Methods: Describe the review protocol — search strategy, inclusion/exclusion criteria, data extraction, and quality assessment methods.
  • Results, Discussion, Conclusion: Present synthesized findings, interpret results in the literature context, discuss implications and biases, and conclude with key takeaways and future needs.
  • References: IEEE style for all citations.

Module 2: Research Concepts & Skills

Core conceptual foundations and practical skills: asking good questions, searching, citing, tools, statistics, and completing research.

2.1 The Concept of Research

Definition, characteristics, types, and the research process

Definition & Characteristics

Research is a quest for understanding — systematic, objective, generating new knowledge, cumulative, and designed to fill gaps between known and unknown.

Types of Research & Articles

  • Types: Experimental, observational, survey, qualitative, quantitative.
  • Article types: Research (original findings) vs. Review (syntheses of existing work).

The Research Process

Typical steps: identifying research questions → literature review → study design → data collection → data analysis → interpretation & reporting.

2.2 Formulating a Research Question

SMART, FINER, FINERMAPS, and refinement techniques

Significance & Process

Research questions (RQs) provide focus and direction, articulate purpose and significance, and must be testable and measurable. Develop RQs by selecting subjects of interest, preliminary reading, and narrowing scope.

Criteria

  • SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.
  • FINER: Feasible, Interesting, Novel, Ethical, Relevant.
  • FINERMAPS: Feasible, Interesting, Novel, Ethical, Relevant, Measurable, Appropriate, Parsimonious, Significant.

Refinement

Refine through preliminary reading to identify debates, and seek feedback from mentors/peers for revision.

2.3 Using Trusted & Scholarly Sources

How to recognize and use trustworthy academic materials

Trusted Resources vs Non-scholarly

  • Trusted = peer-reviewed journal articles, reputable books, academic publishers.
  • Potentially appropriate = scholarly books, encyclopedias (use carefully), institutional reports.
  • Usually inappropriate = personal blogs, non-reviewed websites, general magazines for evidence.

Finding & Evaluating Sources

  • Use databases: Google Scholar, PubMed, JSTOR, ScienceDirect.
  • Evaluate books and journals (publisher reputation, peer review).
  • Avoid untrustworthy or pirated sources; use tools like Unpaywall to find legal PDFs.

2.4 Searching Effectively

Practical search strategies, operators and ethical considerations

Search Strategy

  • Start broad to gain context, then refine with keywords and Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT).
  • Use wildcards: asterisk (*) for root variations, question mark (?) for single-character variations.
  • Choose tools: Google Scholar, PubMed, JSTOR, ScienceDirect; explore AI assistants (Elicit, Research Rabbit) for discovery.

Google Operators & Ethics

  • Useful operators: site:, filetype:, inurl:, "exact phrase", after:YYYY-MM-DD, before:YYYY-MM-DD.
  • Ethical considerations: avoid pirated copies, use Unpaywall or institutional access when available.

2.5 Proper Citation & Academic Integrity

Why citations matter, types of plagiarism, and how to cite correctly

Why Cite?

Citation signals sources of ideas and evidence — it's fundamental for academic integrity, responsibility, and trust.

Plagiarism: Types & Consequences

  • Identity/Complete Plagiarism, Direct Plagiarism, Paraphrasing Plagiarism, Recycling (self-plagiarism), Source-based, Mosaic, and Accidental Plagiarism.
  • Consequences: academic penalties, reputation damage, legal issues.

How to Avoid Plagiarism

  • Always cite sources and use quotations when necessary.
  • Paraphrase correctly and record sources while researching.
  • Use plagiarism checkers to interpret similarity scores (ideal 5–25% small similarity depending on discipline).

Citation Style & Tools

  • YRJ uses IEEE style for in-text citations and references.
  • Tools: Zotero, Mendeley (citation management). Use caution relying on auto-generated citations without checking format.

2.6 Essential Research Software

Tools for citation, typesetting, simulation, and plagiarism checks

Tool Types

  • Simulation / Field tools: AutoCAD, Gambit, Aspen Plus (use field-specific software as needed).
  • Research assistant tools: Zotero (bibliography management), citation plug-ins, proofreading tools.
  • Typesetting: LaTeX (recommended for complex math/technical papers) — Overleaf (online) or TeXStudio (desktop).
  • Plagiarism checkers: Use them to confirm originality and interpret similarity scores responsibly.

LaTeX Basics (Optional)

  • Create a first document: documentclass, begin/end document, maketitle.
  • Text formatting: \textbf{}, \textit{}, lists (itemize, enumerate).
  • Bibliography: thebibliography, \bibitem, \cite commands (or BibTeX / BibLaTeX).

2.7 Research Paper Structure

What to include in each major section of a paper

Key Sections

  • Title Page — title, authors, affiliations, corresponding author details, optional running header, keywords.
  • Abstract — standalone 150–300 words summarizing what, why, how, and key results.
  • Introduction — topic, background, proposed problem, objectives and structure map.
  • Literature Review — define topic, search peer-reviewed literature, outline gaps.
  • Methods — “How you reached findings” (details omitted earlier but include protocol, instruments, analyses).

2.9 Abstract vs Introduction vs Conclusion

Clear distinctions and purposes

Abstract

Standalone summary with core purpose, methods, findings and significance.

Introduction

Background information, objectives, research problem and the roadmap for the paper.

Conclusion

Summarize contributions, practical/theoretical implications and future research directions.

2.10 Making a Plan & Monitoring Progress

Project planning and milestone tracking

Planning

  • Create realistic timelines and Gantt-style milestones for each research phase.
  • Identify deliverables (protocol, data collection, draft, final manuscript).

Monitoring

  • Track progress via Trello, Notion or similar tools; set weekly goals and retrospectives.
  • Keep logs of data collection and decision rationales for reproducibility.

2.11 Executing Your Methodology

From obtaining results to reporting them correctly

Obtaining Results

Careful data collection, documentation of instruments and protocols, ensuring ethical approvals when needed.

Reporting Results

  • Use clear tables, figures and charts with captions and labels.
  • Report descriptive and inferential statistics as appropriate and include caveats.

2.12 Peer Review

Conceptual understanding and practical applications

Concept

Peer review is an essential quality control step — independent assessment by experts to improve clarity, rigour and trustworthiness.

Applications

  • How to review: constructive feedback, checking methods, reproducibility, and citation completeness.
  • How to respond: address reviewer comments professionally, make tracked changes, and provide a response letter.

2.13 Finalizing Your Paper

Proofreading, formatting and submission compliance

Final Steps

  • Proofread thoroughly (grammar, data consistency, figure captions).
  • Format per YRJ template and IEEE style; check all citations match the reference list.
  • Prepare submission packages (manuscript, text-only, figures-only) and any required cover letters.

Module 3: Research in Competitions

How to identify the right competitions and represent your project effectively.

3.1 Knowing the Right Competitions

Selecting platforms that match your topic and level

Identifying Competitions

  • Choose competitions aligned with research scope, stage (student/undergrad/postgrad), and novelty.
  • Check eligibility, deadlines, submission requirements, and judging criteria.

3.2 Summarizing & Representing Your Project

Pitch, poster, and summary best practices

Presenting Your Research

  • Write concise summaries and abstracts tailored to judges or reviewers.
  • Design posters with clear visuals: aim for one key message per panel, legible fonts, and labelled figures.
  • Prepare short pitches (1–3 minutes) highlighting problem, method, result, and impact.

Module 4: What After That? — Next Steps

Opportunities after finishing a research project and how to grow as a researcher.

Publishing & Career Development

Journals, conferences, portfolio building, and collaborations

Publishing Opportunities

  • Target appropriate journals or conferences that match your topic and novelty.
  • Prepare cover letters, follow journal instructions, and be open to revisions.

Research Portfolio & Collaboration

  • Keep a living portfolio: preprints, posters, datasets, code, and talks.
  • Seek collaborations, mentorship, and advanced training (courses, labs, internships).
